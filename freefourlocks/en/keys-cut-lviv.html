Keys to cylinder locks

Locks with cylinder mechanisms are most often installed on entrance doors of apartments and houses, and security is provided by a code cylinder (core). Keys to cylinder locks are easy to recognize: this is a flat plate with wavy cutting or point indentations on one or both sides. Depending on the profile and method of applying the code combination, cylinder keys are divided into two types:

Keys for dimple locks

Keys for dimple locks are also called "euro keys", "laser keys", "perforated keys" - they have notches in the form of dimples or holes of different depths on both sides of the profile, which allows inserting the key into the cylinder opening from either side.

Keys for dimple locks are made from durable alloys, which ensures their longevity and wear resistance. Among them are brass, nickel-plated steel and nickel silver.

Keys for pin tumbler locks

Its profile looks like a plate with wavy indentations and protrusions applied to it. The code cutting visually resembles saw teeth. Such teeth can be present on one side of the key or simultaneously on both sides.

Code pins located inside the cylinder mechanism, after inserting the key into the plug, are positioned between the wavy teeth on the key, aligning and allowing the mechanism to rotate.

Disc tumbler keys

Keys for disc tumbler locks got their "Finnish" name from the country where they were first developed and put into production by the company Abloy. Visually, the key has a semicircular shape and code cuts positioned at various angles. Unlike traditional pin tumbler mechanisms, disc tumbler cylinders differ in their code elements. Inside a disc tumbler cylinder, you'll find discs that are set by the key into specific code positions, matching the cuts on the key. Thanks to these discs, these cylinders offer increased resistance to intelligent picking.

Locks with semicircular, Finnish keys were widespread even during the time of the Soviet Union, and people commonly referred to their keys as "push-button lock keys."

Among manufacturers of cylindrical mechanisms with disc-based secrecy systems on the Ukrainian market, cylinders under the brands "Tokoz" and "Abloy" are most commonly found. In addition to these, companies "Abus," "Lob," and "Sherlock" also use disc-based secrets in their padlocks.

Flat warded keys

Despite the simplicity of their design, warded locks remain popular due to their affordability and ease of installation. Such locks are installed on entrance hall doors, basement doors, garages, or commercial property doors. Warded lock keys are distinguished by their unusual construction: a long blade, either round or flat, with a coded cut on one or both sides at a specific angle. Due to the non-standard nature of the keys, not every workshop undertakes the production of duplicates for warded locks.

Making keys using a code card

To create duplicates of keys for modern locking systems, especially those with enhanced security, a special code card is often required. This is a card you receive with the new lock core or when purchasing a modern lever lock.

It serves two main functions:

Verification of ownership: the card confirms that you are the legitimate owner of the lock and, accordingly, have the right to order a duplicate.

Protection against unauthorized copying: the presence of the card is a mandatory condition for making a copy at authorized centers.

This effectively prevents the creation of keys without the owner's knowledge and allows for the control of duplicate circulation.

Deposit Box Key Duplication

In bank safe deposit boxes, a two-key system is traditionally used: one key is the bank's service key, and the second is the client's key. It's important to emphasize that a duplicate is made exclusively for the client's key, as the bank does not keep copies of client keys.

Two-key locks are typically installed on safe deposit box doors, usually of the lever type. The keys for these are made using the same key duplication equipment as safe keys.

Among the most common manufacturers of lock systems are the aforementioned Mauer and STUV. Both brands specialize in producing mechanisms widely used in the banking sector, particularly for equipping safes and safe deposit boxes.

Also worth noting is the American company Sargent & Greenleaf (S&G), whose depository locks are easily identified by their characteristic flat lever keys.

Additionally, two-key safe deposit box locks from ABLOY can be found on the market, which utilize their recognizable half-round keys for cylinders with a disc mechanism.
