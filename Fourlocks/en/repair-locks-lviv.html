Lever lock repair

Lever tumbler locks are often used on metal doors, in apartments, garages, and safes. They gained popularity in the mid-1990s as primary mortise locks offering increased security.

You'll find them in metal doors from budget brands like Class, Elbor, Pro-Sam, Border, Guardian, and Kale. They also appear in more reliable Italian-made counterparts with improved performance and higher security, such as Cisa, Mottura, Sub, Atra, Iseo, and Securemme.

The springs on the lock's levers are broken

A spring is a thin, flexible wire on each lever (code plate) that helps it return to its original position. Because the levers are constantly moving up and down, these springs eventually crack. When a spring breaks, that lever can't go back to its correct spot after you turn the key, which means the lock won't open and you'll be locked out.

This common problem is found in older models like «Klass», «Elbor», «ZGS (Grodno)», and sometimes even in «Guardian».

After a lock with this problem is opened through emergency means, the individual broken springs on the levers can be replaced. Alternatively, a new set of levers (code plates) can be installed, which will come with a new set of keys.

Key won't open the lever lock

A common reason your own key won't open the lock is something called a "rollover." This is a specific technical issue with the key and/or the lock that prevents the levers inside the lock from lifting correctly.

To clarify, a key for this type of lock has two bittings (code cuts) on both sides. The purpose of these bittings is to correctly position the lock's levers with every half-turn of the key.

When working correctly, during the first half-turn, the key interacts with the code plates (levers), setting them to the correct code position. This allows the bolt (deadbolt/latch) to move in the direction the key is turning. On the second half-turn, the other side of the code bitting interacts with the levers, aligning them into the correct code position again, which also causes the bolt to move further.

Regarding a "rollover," during the first half-turn, the key correctly aligns the levers, and it rotates, but the bolt (deadbolt/latch) doesn't move. As a result, on the second half-turn, the key's second bitting can't set the levers to the correct code position because they're still in the "one" code position, set for the first bitting.

In such a situation, you won't be able to open the door because the key can't "dial in" the correct code combination. To get inside, you'll need to contact a specialist for emergency lock opening.

«Rollover» is a common issue found in budget models like «Class», «Elbor», «Border», «Gardian», and in Italian «Cisa» locks.

Once the lock is unlocked, it needs to be replaced with a new one, as there's a risk of the "rollover" situation happening again.

Wear on the lock levers

Over time, the working edges of the levers, where the key's bitting slides inside the lock, wear down. This means the metal at these contact points gets thinner. Initially, this causes the key to stick when operating the lock, and eventually, the lock will stop opening completely.

Generally, in such cases, the entire set of levers (code plates) in the lock is replaced. Or, if it's a cheap lock model, you'll need to buy a similar replacement model with the same dimensions.

One of the main types of locks commonly used by our citizens is the lock with a removable cylinder mechanism.

Cylinder mechanisms are available in various price segments, from well-known manufacturers like «Mul-T-lock», «Cisa», «Mottura», «Iseo», «Securemme», «Abloy», «Tokoz», «Tesa», «Abus», «Dom», «Evva», «Wilka», and others. With these, problems rarely arise after years of reliable service.

But there are also cheap, low-quality, unnamed models with English or dimple key profiles that were widely imported into our country from China.

Situations where the key gets stuck in the lock or you can't open the lock with your key happen quite often. This usually occurs when a cheap security mechanism is installed in the door, where parts made from low-quality materials quickly fail.

Wear of code pins in cylinder mechanisms

Inside the cylinder are code pins of different heights, which the key contacts upon insertion. Due to friction against the key and each other, these pins wear down, losing their original code dimensions and, along with them, the security of the entire mechanism.

Initially, when this problem occurs, the key starts to turn poorly inside, and eventually, it jams. At a certain point, it won't be able to open the door and let you into your home.

This problem is very rare with expensive cylinder mechanisms, where the pins are made from various wear-resistant alloys. However, it's an almost constant problem with Chinese models, which use brass pins.

Replacing the pins in cheap cylinders isn't cost-effective. It's better to get a replacement cylinder of the same length from a higher price segment. Such a cylinder will offer significantly better security and last much longer.

However, with expensive cylinders from brands like «Mul-T-lock», «Cisa», «Mottura», «Tesa», «Abus», «Dom», and «Wilka», where the code elements are made from very high-quality metals, wear on the key itself is more common after years of use. After a technician diagnoses the cylinder, a new duplicate key is made for it.

Wear cam and coupling of cylinder.

The cam is a component located in the middle of the cylinder mechanism that moves the lock's bolt when you open or close it. To move the cam, you need to insert the "original" key into the cylinder. The key will align the pins in the correct code sequence and push the coupling into the cam.

The coupling is an internal component of the cylinder's security mechanism. It engages with the cam when the cylinder's plug is turned by the key, transferring the rotational force to the cam, which then allows it to move the lock's bolt.

Have you noticed that your key spins freely in the cylinder, but the door bolt doesn't move in either the opening or closing direction? This is one sign that the cam or coupling has failed. Because of this, the rotational force from the key isn't being transferred to the lock's bolt.

If you're unlucky enough to experience this while trying to open your door, and you can't get into your home, you'll have to call a locksmith. They will either drill out or break the lock's cylinder and open your door.

If you discover this problem, you should reach out to us, and we can replace the coupling or cam that has worn out.

Leading brands like «Mul-T-lock», «Evva», «Abus», and «Keso» use stronger materials (steel, brass) for their cams and couplings. This reduces the risk of failure compared to the silumin parts found in budget alternatives.

If a cheap model malfunctions, it's best to simply replace it with a new cylinder mechanism to avoid finding yourself locked out.

Re-keying pin tumbler locks

You can also contact us for re-keying your cylinder security mechanism. Modern patented cylinder mechanisms from brands like «Mul-T-lock», «Abloy», «Tokoz», «Abus», «Viro», «Dom», and «Wilka» have a very important advantage: they can be re-keyed.

Re-keying means changing only the code elements within the cylinder without physically replacing the casing and the plug (the part turned by the key). The main goal of re-keying is to invalidate the old set of keys. Consequently, a new set of keys is made for the updated code combination. This way, your old key will no longer be able to open your lock, and the new set will become your working keys.

Or a "Master System" is created – this is when several cylinders are configured to operate with the same key. All of them have an identical code combination, allowing the customer to open all doors where these cylinders are installed with a single key.
